TITLE TO BE DISCUSSED

A data story on how care work is undervalued and unpaid in the United States.

Imagine you are a baby. You are dependent on the people around you to provide you with food, protection, and affection. That is not only the case for babies, but for all of us. To survive and thrive, we depend on care. Adults too are constantly in need of affection from loved ones, care when sick, or guidance in life. Care work for newborns, adults, and the elderly is the most important foundation of society. Simply put, without care, there is no life.

At four or more children, the mother works 28 hours more than a father every week. That is four more hours every day!

The mother's care hours rise proportionally to the number of children, while the father's remain stagnant, regardless of the number of children.

Oftentimes, women are seen as the natural caregiver. After all, since the beginning of time, and across many cultures, mothers have been humanities primary carers. Originally, those reasons were biological. Only people with the female reproductive system can birth, and later breastfeed children. However, after a child's first year, there are no biological advantages that make mothers better equipped to caring than fathers. The gendered division of care labor nowadays is arbitrary and based on social norms. To better understand the nature of mother's care work, let's break down her total workload into tasks.

All of these tasks are vital for a childs wellbeing or survival. Yet this work is completely unpaid.

The problem is that care-work is still not appreciated by society.

We have seen that mothers are the essential force behind raising and sustaining the entire human race — if women stop, everything stops. Their work should be a source of power and pride, deserving of significant recognition and reward. In reality, mothers' work is entirely unpaid. Care work remains invisible, unglamorous, and underappreciated, leaving women economically disadvantaged.

'Worth' is an opinion, not a fact, and opinions are formed by culture. We exist in a wage-dominated, male-centric culture. Historically, a small group of wealthy white men held power—men who neither engage in nor value care work.

This undervaluing of care work extends beyond the home and into the labor market. Many domestic care tasks—childcare, cooking, cleaning—have paid market equivalents, such as nannies, food servers, and cleaners. Let's explore how paid care work in the labor maket compares to other work.

These changes significantly changed how we spend our time these days.

As a consequence, women work more total hours than men.

Still, women earn less money because the majority of their labor is unwaged.

In our capitalist society, income is directly tied to social power, determining how we live, who we live with, and the terms of our existence. Maternity should not be an unaffordable luxury that either impoverishes women or makes them financially dependent on men. This raises a critical question: Should caregivers be entitled to financial recognition for their work?

Unless care is adequately funded, women will continue to be exploited as an unwaged, unnoticed workforce. The culture surrounding work must be overhauled to value and respect care work. This includes the societal norms around parenting. Caring should be a central aim of society, not an afterthought. This shift is possible. It is a matter of priorities, not resources.
